Astrobiology

Published on: Aug 16, 2024

Tags: Astrobiology, Biology

The term astrobiology was first proposed by the Russian astronomer Gavriil Tikhov in 1953. It is etymologically derived from the Greek ἄστρον, “star”; βίος, “life”; and -λογία, -logia, “study” [1]. Even so, until 1995, the modern use of 'astrobiology' was newly introduced by Wes Huntress and became popular.

NASA has defined astrobiology as "the study of the origin, evolution, and distribution of life in the universe". But to my own, it has a little difference, but the same pursuits: astrobiology is "a branch of science that studies the origin and evolution of life on Earth and finds possible life that may happen elsewhere".

Associated with the popularity of Astrobiology in the 1990s, I found some events that coincided to facilitate its:

  • In 1996, a team of scientists led by David McKay of NASA's JSFC announced that they had discovered evidence for microscopic fossil life in a meteorite from Mars. This meteorite was ejected from Mars 16 million years ago when an asteroid or comet collided with the planet. This 1.93-kilogram rock then moved in an elliptical orbit around the Sun until it was swept up by the Earth 13,000 years ago. Chemical analysis suggests that it originated on Mars when there was liquid water (essential for life) on the planet's surface about 4 billion years ago [2].
  • There're many interesting things that were founded in that Martian meteorite, especially magnetite (Fe3O4), ion sulfide (FeS), PAHs (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons), carbonate globules, etc. For magnetite and ion sulfide, the evidence is quite substantial. It's size and shape look closely like the products of bacteria's metabolism on earth [3][4]. PAHs are a little hard to check out, because they can be interpreted without recourse to biology. PAHs are known to be the most common and abundant in the visible universe, including in comets or formed within giant interstellar clouds of gas and dust. More than 20% of the carbon in the universe may be associated with PAHs, possible starting materials for the formation of life. PAHs seem to have been formed as early as a couple of billion years after the Big Bang [5][6].
  • The PAH world hypothesis proposes PAHs played a major role in the origin of life by mediating the synthesis of RNA molecules, leading into the RNA world. According to this hypothesis, the chemical evolution process that forms life on Earth includes the following stages: Inorganic → Simple organic → PAH → RNA → DNA. From there, primitive cells were formed, and life moved into prebiotic evolution. However, as of yet, the hypothesis is untested.
  • The second thing, In the 1980s and 1990s, biologists found that microbial life has great flexibility for surviving in extreme environments: acidic, extraordinarily hot, or with irregular air pressure. That's the same condition in the universe that we can imagine. These tiny things are called “Extremophiles,” with the main group of Archaea (one of three domains of life in biology, same as bacteria and eukarya)[7]. Studying extreme environments on Earth can help researchers understand the limits of habitability on other worlds. An example from wiki that, analogous deserts in Antarctica are exposed to harmful UV radiation, low temperatures, high salt concentrations, and low mineral concentrations. These conditions are similar to those on Mars. Therefore, finding viable microbes in the subsurface of Antarctica suggests that there may be microbes surviving in endolithic communities and living under the Martian surface.
  • Paracoccus denitrificans. The bacteria were cultivated while being rotated in an ultracentrifuge at high speeds corresponding to 403,627 g (i.e., 403,627 times the gravity experienced on Earth). P. denitrificans was one of the bacteria that displayed not only survival but also robust cellular growth under these conditions of hyperacceleration, which are usually found only in cosmic environments, such as on very massive stars or in the shock waves of supernovas [8][9][10].
  • In September 2015, scientists from CNR-National Research Council of Italy reported that S. soflataricus was able to survive under Martian radiation at a wavelength that was considered extremely lethal to most bacteria. This discovery is significant because it indicates that not only bacterial spores (I call it in a funny way: hibernation bacteria with a thick coat), but also growing cells can be remarkably resistant to strong UV radiation [11].
  • Deinococcus radiodurans were found to survive for three years in outer space, based on studies on the International Space Station [12].
  • Furthermore, the Galileo mission, which launched in 1989 and entered orbit around Jupiter in 1995, provided some images showing that Europa - a moon of Jupiter, has an ice-covered surface. "One of the most important discoveries made by Galileo showed how Jupiter's magnetic field was disrupted in the space around Europa. This measurement strongly implied that a special type of magnetic field is being created (induced) within Europa by a deep layer of some electrically conductive fluid beneath the surface. Based on Europa's icy composition, scientists think the most likely material to create this magnetic signature is a global ocean of salty water." With three requirements for life that Europa seems to be able to provide in the same or other ways as Earth: liquid water, certain chemical elements, and an energy source, Europa's ocean became one of the most promising places to took for life beyond Earth [3].
  • Then, since 1992, the first recognized and increasing numbers of exoplanets were found by astronomers, which are outside our Solar System and orbit other stars not the Sun. "The discovery of exoplanets has increased interest in the search for extraterrestrial life. There is special interest in planets that orbit in a star's habitable zone (or sometimes called "Goldilocks zone"), where it is possible for liquid water, a prerequisite for life as we know it, to exist on the surface."
